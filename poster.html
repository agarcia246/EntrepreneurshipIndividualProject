Building My Own Solution: Three Attempts to Create a Personal Fitness & Wellness Tracker That Actually Sticks

Alex Antonio Garcia Perdriau

Initiative Resourcefulness Resilience

Entrepreneurial Mindset Traits

My Challenge

Build a personal fitness and wellness tracking app from scratch and use it consistently for two weeks to measurably improve my health and daily routine, while at the same time testing it and refining it in order to see how usefull it would be for a larger market.

Initiative

Instead of downloading another app that I would abandon in a week, I decided to build my own tool from scratch, like that I would be personally invested in it and I could better adapt it to my needs.

Resourcefulness

I aimed to challenge myself by learning new technologies in order to widen my skillset. Through this project I ended up learning a lot about backend technologies such as supabase and how I can integrate them successfuly into a working app in order to create a more finished product.

Resilience

Throughout this challenge, I struggled with keeping up with the consistency that I initially set for myself. There were periods where I kept up strongly with the challenge but if I missed one day I would quickly lose motivation and would stuggle to get it back.

Attempt 1: The Over-Engineered Start

Traits Targeted

Initiative, resourcefulness

Goal

Build a complete fitness planner app and use it daily for one week to track workouts, meals, and habits.

What I Planned

Design every screen, build them all before starting to use the app.

What Actually Happened

Spent the entire first week designing and building the UI. But everything was hardcoded and I ended up with no real tracking. When I tried to use it, there was nothing to actually log. The data reset every time I restarted the website. I abandoned it after two days of pretending it worked. During this first week I ended up focusing too much on the app and I ended up putting of its actual purpose which was getting me to work out and eat healthier and I ended up only going to the gym for 2 days and filling myself up with junk food.

Outcome

Failure. A good-looking prototype with zero real functionality. I never actually tracked a single workout or meal. No progress on my actual goals of improving my health.

How I felt: Frustrated by how much time I wasted on less important parts of the challenge. I had spent all my energy making something look good instead of making it work. It felt like I was avoiding the hard part and I was wasting my time.

What I learnt: A good interface is useless if I can't apply it to my real objectives.

Attempt 2: Making It Real

Goal

Make the app actually work and implement it into my daily routine consistently.

What I Planned

Add a real database and backend functionality. Focus on making the core functionality of the app work. Start recording my workouts and meals on the app.

What Actually Happened

Set up database for storage. Rebuilt every screen to work with the database. Started using it daily and building a strong routine. I found myself going to the gym more consistently and being more mindful of what I ate. The first few days were going great but on day 4 I had some other issues in my life that occured and I ended up breaking my streak. After this one day turned into two which turned into 4 and by the end of the week I found myself making excuses to stop continuing with my progress.

Outcome

Partial success.I got the app working and it was going really well at first but because of my own faults I wasn't able to keep up.

How I felt: Encouraged that the app worked but mad at myself that even after putting in all of this hard work I still couldn't sustain the habit.

What I learnt: Data capture alone does not drive behaviour change. I needed something else to keep my motivation going.

Attempt 3: The Feedback Loop

Goal

Use the app consistently for a full week. Measure my success by seeing logging as many daily habits as I could.

What I Planned

Add features to the app such as streaks and notifications that can call my attention to the app and reward my consistency.

What Actually Happened

Added push notifications that would message me every day at 11 to go the gym, at 3 to record my meal and at 9 to record my dinner. Deployed it live and it worked for a bit but then the notification server crashed and I wasn't able to get it back up working. Also added a streaks feature that would help highlight my activity. Had a rough thursday where I didn't go to the gym, but I was able to recover by Friday. By the end of the week, I had logged my data correctly and I was happy with my consistency.

Outcome

Meaningful progress: Habit completion improved over the week. The tool became something I genuinely opened every morning. Not perfect but worked well enough.

How I felt: Proud, not because the app was perfect, but because I had built something that actually changed my daily behaviour.

Final Reflection

When I chose this challenge, I assumed the hard part would be the code. I was wrong. The hardest part was confronting my own habits with real data and holding myself accountable to my goal. Building a fitness tracker for myself sounded simple, but it required me to put in constant effort to make sure that I kept up with my goal in time.

Initiative meant starting from zero on a new venture instead of settling for an existing app I already knew I would abandon the second I found the smallest issue. Resourcefulness meant teaching myself new technologies through countless hours of reading reading documentation and fighting with chatgpt to fix bugs in the code. Resilience was tested every time a version of the app failed to change my behaviour. Attempt 1 produced a beautiful but useless product which distracted me from my main goal. Attempt 2 let me finish my product but not using it towards my goal. Each time, I had to resist the urge to declare the whole idea a failure and looking for another project instead of thinking: How can I move on from this issue.

I never published this app or sought external users. The challenge was entirely personal: could I build something that changed my own daily routine? The answer, after three attempts, is a qualified yes. My habits are not perfect, but they are tracked, visible, and improving. I now understand that resilience is not about never failing, it is about using failure as our base for the next success.
