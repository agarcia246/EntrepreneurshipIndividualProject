Building Less, Listening More: Three Attempts to Test a Student Fitness and Healthy-Eating Planner

Alex Antonio Garcia Perdriau
Initiative Humility Uncertainty

My Chosen Entrepreneurial Mindset Traits

Initiative

Nothing in this challenge would happen unless I actively approached people, asked for feedback, and followed up instead of waiting for the right moment.

Humility

I wanted to get better at hearing honest feedback without becoming defensive, especially when other students did not value the idea in the way I expected.

Comfort with Uncertainty

I had no way of knowing whether strangers would ignore the tool, criticise it, or actually use it, so I wanted to practice acting without certainty.

Attempt 1: Starting Point

Traits Targeted

Initiative, humility, comfort with uncertainty

Goal

Test whether a student-built planner combining gym routines and healthier eating could solve a real problem. Target: 8 students to try it, 2 to use it for a full week.

Planned Approach

Build a polished prototype first, then share it online and let the quality of the idea speak for itself.

What Actually Happened

Spent too much time polishing the tool before speaking to real users. Built the homepage, weekly tracker, and meal-planning section, then posted a generic message in student group chats and Instagram with a form link. Avoided approaching people in person.

Outcome

Failure. A few clicks, one incomplete response. No one used it for a week. No detailed feedback.

Felt: Embarrassed, frustrated, disappointed — realised I had hidden behind building instead of testing.

Learned: A polished idea ≠ a wanted idea. Silence is still feedback. Generic outreach teaches very little.

Attempt 2: Adapted Plan

Goal

Same: 8 students to try it, 2 to use it for a full week.

Planned Approach

Simplify the prototype, speak to students in person, and ask for direct feedback instead of hoping a link would do the work.

What Actually Happened

Reduced the prototype to one simple weekly view. Prepared three short questions about gym habits, eating struggles, and whether the tool seemed useful. Approached students near the library and student union. Had real conversations and gathered honest reactions.

Outcome

Partial success. Spoke to more students face-to-face. Received useful criticism. Some were interested but most didn't follow through for a full week.

Felt: More engaged but still uncomfortable. The in-person conversations were harder than expected, yet more rewarding.

Learned: Real feedback requires real contact. Simplification made the idea easier to explain and test. Discomfort is part of the process.

Attempt 3: Final Iteration

Goal

Refined: focus less on sign-ups and more on understanding what students actually needed from a wellness tool.

Planned Approach

Lead with questions, not the product. Use feedback from Attempt 2 to reshape the pitch and the tool itself. Follow up with interested students.

What Actually Happened

Shifted from selling to listening. Asked open-ended questions first, only showing the tool if relevant. Followed up via messages with students who expressed interest. Created a simplified version based on the most common pain points heard.

Outcome

Meaningful progress. Deeper conversations. A few students used the tool for multiple days. The idea evolved based on genuine needs rather than assumptions.

Felt: More confident and less attached to being right. The discomfort became productive rather than paralysing.

Learned: Listening first builds trust and reveals what matters. Iteration is not failure — it's the method. The idea improved because I let go of my first version.

Final Reflection

This challenge taught me that entrepreneurial thinking is not about having a perfect idea — it is about being willing to expose an imperfect one to the world and learn from what comes back. Across three attempts, I moved from hiding behind a polished prototype to having genuine conversations with strangers about their real problems.

The traits I chose — initiative, humility, and comfort with uncertainty — were not just labels. They were muscles I had to use repeatedly. Initiative meant sending that first awkward message and walking up to a stranger in the library. Humility meant accepting that my idea needed changing. Comfort with uncertainty meant continuing to act even when I had no guarantee the next attempt would go better.

The biggest shift was internal: I stopped trying to prove my idea was good and started trying to understand whether it was useful. That reframe changed everything — from how I asked questions to how I interpreted silence. If I were to do this again, I would start with conversations on day one and build only after listening.
